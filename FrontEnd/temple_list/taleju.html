[] Taleju Bhawani Temple

Comments:

Taleju Temple is a Hindu temple dedicated to Taleju Bhawani, the royal goddess of the Malla dynasty of Nepal. It was built in 1564 by Mahendra Malla and is located in Hanuman Dhoka, Kathmandu Durbar Square, a UNESCO World Heritage Site. Inside the temple, there is a shire dedicated to Taleju Bhawani, and Kumari Devi. Taleju Temple is only opened once a year on the occasion of Dashain.



History

The Taleju Temple was originally constructed in the 14th century during the reign of King Sthiti Malla, who ruled over the Kathmandu Valley. It was built in the traditional Newar architectural style, characterized by intricately carved woodwork and pagoda-style architecture. The temple was dedicated to Goddess Taleju, a Hindu deity believed to be an incarnation of Goddess Durga. Taleju is considered the patron goddess of the Malla kings who ruled the Kathmandu Valley.



Spiritual Significance

The Taleju Temple held immense religious significance during the Malla dynasty's rule. It was considered the state temple and the place where the Malla kings received the goddess's blessings. It served as a focal point for various religious rituals, ceremonies, and festivals, playing a central role in the spiritual and cultural life of the valley.



Temple Opening Mystery

One of the intriguing aspects of the Taleju Temple's history is that it was traditionally open to the public only once a year during the Indra Jatra festival. According to local legend, the goddess Taleju appeared in the form of a bee and would leave the temple if anyone entered during the rest of the year. In 1956, King Mahendra broke this tradition by entering the temple during a crisis. Since then, the temple has remained open for occasional visitors.



Ritual

The Taleju Temple, a significant religious structure in Kathmandu, Nepal, is associated with various rituals and ceremonies, many of which are deeply rooted in Hindu traditions. While access to the temple is restricted for much of the year, some rituals and festivals provide opportunities for devotees and visitors to witness the religious activities at the temple. Here are some of the notable rituals and festivals associated with the Taleju Temple:

1. Indra Jatra Festival:
Indra Jatra is one of the most famous festivals in Kathmandu Valley and has a strong connection to the Taleju Temple. The festival typically occurs in late August or early September and lasts for eight days. During this festival, the Kumari Jatra (procession of the Living Goddess Kumari) takes place, where the Kumari (a young girl revered as a living goddess) makes appearances from the Kumari Ghar to bless the people. One of the most significant events is the raising of the ceremonial lingo (a ceremonial pole) in front of the Taleju Temple, symbolizing the worship of Lord Indra, the god of rain.

2. Dasain Festival:
Dasain, also known as Vijaya Dashami, is the biggest Hindu festival in Nepal, celebrated during the lunar month of Ashwin (usually in September or October). It is a time for family reunions and religious ceremonies. Devotees visit the Taleju Temple to seek the goddess's blessings during this festival.

3. Sweta Machhendranath Jatra:
The Sweta Machhendranath Jatra is an annual chariot procession in Kathmandu that involves the deity Sweta Machhendranath (a form of Lord Shiva) being paraded through the city. The procession begins and ends at the temple of Sweta Machhendranath, which is believed to be closely linked to the Taleju Temple.

4. Other Occasional Rituals:
Apart from these major festivals, occasional rituals and pujas (prayer ceremonies) are conducted at the Taleju Temple, especially during auspicious lunar or astrological events. The temple is typically closed to the public, and access is restricted. Devotees visit during specific religious occasions to offer prayers and seek blessings.
