[] Kumari Ghar

Comments:

Kumari Ghar, located in Kathmandu Durbar Square, holds a unique cultural significance as the residence of the Kumari, a living goddess revered in Newari Hindu tradition. The historic building, with its intricately carved wooden architecture, serves as the Kumari's dwelling, where she resides until her adolescence. The Kumari is chosen through a sacred process and is considered the earthly embodiment of the goddess Taleju. Visitors to Kumari Ghar experience the rich blend of spirituality and artistry, witnessing traditional Newari craftsmanship in the architecture and having the rare chance to catch a glimpse of the Kumari during occasional public appearances.



History

Kumari Ghar, situated in Kathmandu Durbar Square, has a rich history dating back to the 18th century. Built by King Jaya Prakash Malla, this historic palace served as the residence for the Kumari, a young prepubescent girl chosen as the living goddess in the Newar community. The tradition of selecting a Kumari has ancient roots, and the Kumari Ghar has been the focal point of this cultural practice. The palace features intricately carved wooden architecture, reflecting the artistic craftsmanship of the Newar people. The Kumari, revered as the living embodiment of the goddess Taleju, resides in Kumari Ghar until she reaches puberty, after which a new Kumari is selected. This living tradition continues to be a symbol of Nepal's unique cultural heritage.



Festival

Kumari Jatra, which means the chariot festival of Kumari, coincides with Indra Jatra. It was started in 1756 AD during the reign of Jaya Prakash Malla. During this festival, three chariots carrying human representations of the deities Ganesh, Bhairava and Kumari accompanied by musical bands are pulled along the festival route through Kathmandu on three days. The procession starts at around 3 pm.



Significance

Kumari, Kumari Devi, or the Living Goddess is the tradition of worshipping a chosen virgin as manifestations of the divine female energy or Shakti in Dharmic Nepali religious traditions. It is believed that the girl is possessed by the goddess Taleju or Durga. The word Kumari is derived from Sanskrit meaning princess.
